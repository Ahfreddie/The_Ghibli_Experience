Studio Ghibli is one of the world's most iconic animation houses. Established in 1985, this Japanese studio has left a lasting mark with stories that blend breathtaking visuals, masterful storytelling, unforgettable characters, and a wide range of meaningful themes. Even with the rise and dominance of CGI animation, Ghibli has continued to craft its worlds through traditional 2D artistry.
This page is my notebook — a collection of my observations on how Ghibli portrays love through the 2D medium, two forms of art that have become rare in modern animated media. Join me as I explore what makes these 2D love stories stand out from conventional fairytale romances, and how Ghibli approaches them with depth, subtlety, and heart.

Where This Journey Begins

This all started with a conversation — one of those quiet, thoughtful ones — about how Disney shifted from 2D animation into fully embracing 3D, and how love stories in modern Disney films feel different from the classic fairy-tale era. Romance used to be loud, central, and unapologetic. Today, it often feels… careful.

It seems like modern Disney has been intentionally stepping away from the obvious tropes — the love at first sight, the idea that a girl's purpose is fulfilled by Prince Charming. Films like Frozen and Tangled openly poke fun at these ideas: true love's kiss doesn't work the way it once did, and the heroine doesn't automatically fall for the handsome stranger who appears out of nowhere. And while there's nothing wrong with the stories being told today, it personally felt like a hesitation — almost a fear — of telling genuine, grounded love stories where romance isn't secondary or ironic.

Which is interesting, because Disney has done this beautifully before. The Aristocats, for example, tells a warm, simple love story that doesn't try too hard — it just lets the relationship grow naturally.

That's when I started thinking about Studio Ghibli.

At the time, I had only seen a handful of Ghibli films, but every one of them approached love in a quiet, meaningful way. Not loud. Not announced. Just felt. It made me wonder why most Western studios don't approach love like this — stories that focus on connection, trust, and presence. Stories that don't tell us “this is a love story”, but show us through small moments, shared silences, and gentle growth.

A perfect example is Ponyo, where love is explored through the innocence of children. It never over-romanticizes the feeling, yet it clearly shows what matters — care, loyalty, and sincerity — without forcing a message or pushing an agenda.

That, to me, is the magic of Ghibli. The studio tells deeply human stories, often in 2D animation — a medium many major studios have left behind in favor of flashy CGI (which isn't a bad thing). But there's something special about how much emotion, warmth, and meaning Ghibli expresses through this format.

So I decided to explore Ghibli's love stories more intentionally. I made a list of films where love plays a central role, with a simple goal: to truly understand what sets these stories apart, to appreciate the beauty of grounded love, and to see how 2D animation can still be a powerful way to tell stories that matter.

Join me as I share my thoughts, notes, and observations from The Ghibli Experience.
